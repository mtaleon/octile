How to Play

Fill the entire 8×8 board with 11 tiles. Three grey tiles are already placed — arrange the remaining 8 colored tiles to fill every empty cell.

Place a Tile

  • Drag & drop a tile from the piece tray onto the board.
  • Or tap a tile to select it (highlighted in yellow), then tap an empty cell on the board to place it.

Rotate a Tile

  • Tap an already-selected tile again to rotate it 90°.

Remove a Tile

  • Drag a placed tile off the board to return it to the tray.
  • Or tap a placed tile on the board to pick it back up.

Controls

  • # + Go — jump to a specific puzzle (1–11,378).
  • Random — load a random puzzle.
  • Hint — reveal the correct position of one unplaced tile.

Every puzzle has a solution. Take your time — this is about thinking, not speed.

The Story of Octile

In 250 BC, Archimedes devised the Stomachion — a square divided into 14 ivory pieces. He posed a deceptively simple question: in how many distinct ways can these pieces fill the square?

The puzzle consumed scholars for over two millennia. It was only in 2003 that mathematician Bill Cutler, aided by computers, found the answer: 536 unique solutions.

From 14 Pieces to 11

Octile reimagines this ancient challenge on an 8×8 grid. Eleven colored tiles — from a tiny 1×1 square to a massive 3×4 block — must fill all 64 cells. Three grey tiles are placed by fate; the remaining eight are yours to arrange.

Like Archimedes, we asked: how many unique puzzles exist? After exhaustive search with D4 symmetry (rotations and reflections), the answer: 11,378 — every one solvable, every one unique.

Why "Octile"?

Oct (eight) for the 8×8 board. Tile for the ancient art of tessellation. A name that bridges Greek mathematics and a puzzle you hold in your hands today.
